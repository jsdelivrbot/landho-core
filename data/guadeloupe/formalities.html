Clearance

Entry formalities must be completed at one of the official ports. The Q flag must be flown and only the captain may disembark to complete formalities.

All formalities are done by computer now in Guadeloupe, normally located in the marina office, making checking in and out much simpler.

Immigration

All visitors (including French citizens) require a passport valid for 6 months.

As an overseas territory of France, and therefore a member of the EU. Guadeloupe comes under French immigration rules, but is not a member of the Schengen Area Agreement.

For the exact details applicable to Guadeloupe, see the French Embassy website

All other nationalities require visas, which should be obtained from a French embassy or consulate in advance. Ensure that the visa given is valid for Guadeloupe.

Visitors from South America and some Caribbean islands may need a certificate of vaccinations against smallpox and yellow fever.

Last updated October 2015.

Customs

All weapons should be declared.

Guadeloupe is a department of France and similar regulations apply as to France itself.

The level of safety equipment onboard may be checked.

A private yacht may stay in the country for 18 months before becoming liable to tax.

Last updated October 2015.

Health

Tap water is safe to drink. Bottled water is also available.

Guadeloupe is a French Department, therefore the health care system is excellent. Unlike most tropical regions, there are almost no communicable diseases or those transmitted by parasites. There are no poisonous or dangerous animals, except as noted below. Other good advice:

Don't stay under the Manchineel tree (similar to an apple tree). It has caustic and mildly poisonous sap. Don't touch the leaves or eat the fruits. The juices burn the skin. The main island of the Guadeloupe archipelago is cleared of most of these trees, but there are still plenty of them on the Saintes and on Saint-Bart - very often marked with warning signs.

Some fish, especially around the northern islands eat poisoned seaweed which can lead to severe food poisoning in humans.

Swimming in some rivers is dangerous because of Bilharziosis (a parasitic disease). Normally there are warning signs.

Beware of the Scolopendra (millepede with a shell). Its sting is similar to that of a scorpion.

Be aware of stray dogs at the beaches, because they can transmit worms. Wear your sandals to the beach and use a mat.

Some mosquitos transmit the Dengue virus. Sleep under a mosquito net.

Fees

Port tax is payable depending on length of stay and size of yacht. No customs charges are made for EU countries and US yachts, although yachts from some other countries may have to pay some fees calculated by day and tonnage. Normally one only pays and administrative fee (2-5 Euros) at select marinas/cafes with computers.

Restrictions

Chartering is illegal in French territory, although transit with non-French passengers is allowed.

Fishing: Only lines with a limited number of hooks are allowed. The use of nets, harpoons and lobster pots is forbidden. Night fishing with lights is not allowed. The taking of turtles, corals, sponges or sea plants is prohibited and the catching several species of fish is restricted. See Info

The following areas are Maritime Reserves: Grand Cul-de-Sac Marin, Ilet Pigeon (Pointe de Malendure - Pointe Mahault), Petite-Terre, Banc du Crabier ( NE of Desirade).

Pets

Guadeloupe comes under the Pets Travel Scheme (PETS) and animals need a current Health Certificate, and a valid anti-rabies vaccination certificate issued by a Veterinarian from the country of shipment. No dogs or cats under 3 months old allowed.
